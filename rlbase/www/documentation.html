RLBase

RLBase is a web database for accessing and exploring standardized R-loop mapping data from > 600 publicly-available samples. Key features:

  1. Explore hundreds of R-loop mapping datasets
  2. Explore R-loop regions (sites of consensus R-loop formation)
  3. Analyze your own R-loop mapping data
  4. Download processed and standardized data sets.

RLBase is a core component of RLSuite, an R-loop analysis software toolchain. RLSuite also includes RLPipes (a CLI pipeline for upstream R-loop data processing), RLSeq (R package for downstream R-loop data analysis), and RLHub (R package interface to the RLBase data store).

Our goal in this document is to (1) Describe the backround and motivation of RLBase, (2) showcase the intended usage of RLBase, and (3) address FAQs which may arise when using RLBase.

Background and Motivation

R-Loops and R-loop mapping

R-loops are three-stranded nucleic acid structures formed from the hybridization of RNA and DNA, typically during transcription. R-loops have long been studied in the context of pathology, but recent evidence indicates that play a crucial (albeit poorly understood) role in normal cell physiology (Niehrs and Luke 2020).

Depiction of an R-loop and the factors which may promote them (Hegazy et al., 2020; PMID: 31843970)

With the advent of DNA-RNA immunoprecipitation sequencing (DRIP-Seq) in 2012, it become possible to map R-loop locations throughout the genome (Ginno et al. 2012). In the years since, dozens of studies have implemented R-loop mapping approaches and submitted their data to public repositories like the Sequence Read Archive (SRA).

The RLBase project (est. 2019)

We began the RLBase project in 2019 with the aim of standardizing public R-loop mapping samples and making the resulting data accessible for R-loop biologists to explore and analysis. Our first publication from this project used over 100 DRIP-Seq data-sets to reveal an unexpected role for physiological R-loops in chromatin architecture (Pan et al. 2020). Building on this pilot study, we began in 2020 to manually catalog all public R-loop mapping samples, finding > 40 studies and > 600 data sets. We developed and implemented a light-weight CLI pipeline, RLPipes, to standardize the re-processing of these data from raw reads.

Unexpectedly, we found drastic inconsistency in sample data quality, posing a major hurdle to meta-analysis and exploration of these data, echoing the findings of Chedin et al., 2021 (Chédin et al. 2021) found in their meta-analysis.

To overcome this limitation, we developed new methods for assessing sample quality (implemented in the RLSeq R package). We implemented an online-learning scheme and developed an ensemble ML model which accurately categorizes R-loop data sets as “POS” (robustly maps R-loops) or “NEG” (poorly maps R-loops) (see model documentation). By excluding the “NEG” samples, we derived a high-confidence sample set for meta-analysis. In our first meta-analysis, we identified R-loop regions (RL Regions), sites of consensus R-loop formation. We also analyzed the correlation between R-loop signal and expression within RL Regions. These data can be explored in the “RL Regions” panel of “RLBase.”

Usage

RLBase can be used for all of the following:

  • Exploration of R-loop mapping samples (view)
    • Exploration of mapping samples from different modalities
    • Analysis of sample quality
    • Analysis of genomic feature enrichment
  • R-loop Region (RL Region) analysis (link)
    • Exploration of R-loop regions
    • Analysis of R-loop region correlation with gene expression
  • User-supplied samples (view)
    • Analysis of user-supplied data to generate an HTML report.

Analysis of RLBase samples

The samples in RLBase were standardized and reprocessed using the RLPipes CLI tool. They were then analyzed with RLSeq which performed R-loop forming sequences analysis, predict sample quality, analyze genomic feature enrichment, and to calculate their overlap with R-loop regions (RL Regions). The metadata from these analyses along with other sample information are provided in the sample data table and can be explored interactively.
